While the Federal Aviation Administration's Section 2209 NPRM dominates national headlines, Florida has quietly enacted one of the most aggressive state-level drone laws in the country. The recently passed Unmanned Aircraft Systems Act creates felony-level penalties for drone operations near critical infrastructure and establishes new privacy protections that go beyond federal requirements.

This isn't just a state story. It's a preview of how the patchwork of federal and state drone regulations is likely to evolve — and a warning for operators who assume compliance with FAA rules is sufficient.

## What the Florida Law Actually Says

The statute, codified at Section 330.41 of the Florida Statutes, prohibits two categories of drone operations over "critical infrastructure facilities":

**Physical Contact or Interference**
Drone operators may not make contact with critical infrastructure facilities or operate close enough to interfere with or cause disturbance to facility operations. This goes beyond simple overflight restrictions to address operational interference.

**Privacy Violations**
The act prohibits using drones to capture images or recordings in a manner that violates a reasonable expectation of privacy. This creates a privacy overlay that doesn't exist in federal drone regulations, which focus primarily on safety and airspace rather than privacy.

**Remote ID Tampering**
Notably, the statute also prohibits altering a drone in a manner that contravenes FAA Remote ID requirements. This creates state-level enforcement of a federal mandate — a novel approach that could serve as a model for other states.

## Penalties: Serious Business

Violations of Section 330.41(4)(a) can be prosecuted as third-degree felonies under Florida law. This represents a dramatic escalation from typical FAA enforcement, which relies primarily on civil penalties.

The felony classification means:
- Potential prison sentences up to five years
- Fines up to $5,000
- Criminal record implications
- Potential loss of professional licenses for commercial operators

This creates a dual-track enforcement environment where the same drone operation could face civil FAA penalties and criminal state prosecution simultaneously.

## What's Covered: Broad Definition of "Critical Infrastructure"

Florida defines "critical infrastructure facility" expansively, including:

- Correctional institutions and detention centers
- Energy generation, transmission, and distribution facilities
- Chemical manufacturing and storage facilities
- Water treatment and wastewater facilities
- Telecommunications infrastructure
- Certain transportation facilities
- Government buildings (specified categories)

The breadth of covered facilities means operators can't assume familiarity with a location's status. A facility that doesn't appear on federal critical infrastructure lists might still qualify under Florida's broader definition.

## Important Exceptions

The statute isn't absolute. It contains exceptions for:

- Federal, state, and local government entities and contractors
- Law enforcement agencies and persons acting under their direction
- Facility owners, operators, or occupants
- Persons with prior written consent from facility owners

These exceptions preserve legitimate government and commercial operations while targeting unauthorized activity.

## The Federal-State Overlay

Florida's law creates an interesting regulatory dynamic. The FAA's Section 2209 NPRM would establish a federal petition process for flight restrictions over fixed-site facilities. Florida's law creates immediate, automatic restrictions without requiring federal petition or approval.

This means:
- A facility in Florida might have state-level restrictions immediately
- The same facility might simultaneously be pursuing federal UAFR designation
- Operators must comply with whichever standard is more restrictive
- State law enforcement can act without waiting for federal processes

For operators, this creates compliance complexity. A flight plan that clears FAA airspace requirements might still violate state law if it passes over a Florida critical infrastructure facility.

## Why Florida Matters Nationally

Florida isn't an isolated case. It's part of a broader trend:

**State-Level Regulatory Expansion**
Multiple states have enacted or are considering drone restrictions that go beyond federal minimums. Texas, California, and New York have all introduced legislation addressing drones near critical infrastructure, though Florida's felony penalties are among the most severe.

**Privacy as a Regulatory Driver**
While federal drone regulation has focused on safety and airspace integration, state laws increasingly address privacy concerns. Florida's reasonable expectation of privacy standard creates a subjective test that will require case-by-case interpretation.

**Criminalization Trend**
The shift from civil to criminal penalties represents a significant escalation. Where the FAA typically fines violators, states can imprison them. This changes the risk calculus for operators dramatically.

## Compliance Implications

For commercial operators working in Florida, the new law requires:

**Enhanced Pre-Flight Planning**
Operators must identify not just federally restricted airspace but also state-defined critical infrastructure facilities. This requires access to state-level facility databases that may not be as readily available as FAA airspace information.

**Client Verification**
Commercial operators conducting infrastructure inspections must verify they have proper authorization — either as contractors to facility owners or with explicit written consent. Assumptions about authorization could lead to felony charges.

**Insurance Review**
Commercial insurance policies may need updating to address criminal liability exposure. Standard drone liability policies typically cover civil penalties and damages, not criminal defense costs.

**Cross-State Operational Complexity**
For operators working across multiple states, Florida's law adds another variable to an already complex regulatory landscape. Each state may have different definitions, restrictions, and penalties.

## Bottom Line

Florida's Unmanned Aircraft Systems Act represents the leading edge of state-level drone regulation. While the FAA's Section 2209 NPRM works through federal processes, states aren't waiting — they're creating their own frameworks with broader definitions and harsher penalties.

For drone operators, the message is clear: federal compliance is necessary but not sufficient. State laws can impose additional restrictions with criminal consequences, and the patchwork of state regulations is only getting more complex.

The industry has long called for regulatory clarity. Florida's law suggests that clarity may come not as a single federal standard, but as a complex web of overlapping federal and state requirements that operators must navigate carefully.

For now, operators working in Florida should review their flight planning procedures, verify insurance coverage, and stay current on both federal and state requirements. The cost of confusion just got significantly higher.
