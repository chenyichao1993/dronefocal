When DJI Agriculture unveiled its fifth annual industry report at Agrishow 2026 in Ribeirão Preto, Brazil, the numbers told a story that would have seemed impossible just a decade ago: **over 600,000 agricultural drones** now operate across more than 100 countries and regions, saving approximately **410 million tons of water** and cutting **51 million tons of carbon emissions** annually.

To put that in perspective, the water savings alone equal the annual drinking water consumption of roughly 740 million people. The carbon reduction is equivalent to the annual absorption capacity of 240 million trees. These aren't marketing projections — they're operational results from a technology that has graduated from experimental novelty to essential farm equipment.

## The Brazil Showcase

Brazil isn't just another market on DJI's global map. It's become a proving ground for what agricultural drones can achieve at scale, across diverse crop types, and under real commercial pressure.

DJI's agricultural drones — including the **Agras T25P, T70P, and T100** — are now deployed across Brazil's major agricultural sectors: coffee, soybeans, corn, sugarcane, and forage grass. The applications go far beyond simple crop dusting. Brazilian operators are using drones for full-cycle precision operations: seeding, spot-spraying, nutrient application, and pasture management.

The results are measurable and significant. In forage management, drone-based spot-spraying of weed patches has reduced herbicide use by up to **35%** compared to blanket application methods. Full-process drone spraying and seeding eliminate soil compaction from heavy ground equipment, reduce chemical drift near sensitive ecosystems, and lower the overall carbon footprint of livestock farming operations.

"Agricultural drones are no longer a novelty — they are essential farm equipment worldwide," said Yuan Zhang, Head of Global Sales at DJI Agriculture, at the Brazil launch event. "In Brazil, DJI drones are now widely applied on the country's major crops. As global adoption continues to grow, we will strengthen our support network while offering training through over 7,000 certified instructors globally."

## The Numbers Behind the Transformation

DJI's 2025/2026 Agricultural Drone Industry Insight Report, the fifth in an annual series, provides the most comprehensive public dataset on agricultural drone adoption available. Key findings include:

- **600,000+ DJI agricultural drones** in active service by end of 2025
- **600,000+ trained operators** certified and operating globally
- **3,500 service and repair centers** in DJI Agriculture's worldwide network
- **7,000+ certified instructors** providing training across markets
- **410 million tons of water saved** — equivalent to annual drinking water for 740 million people
- **51 million tons of carbon emissions cut** — equivalent to annual carbon absorption by 240 million trees

These figures represent a maturing industry with measurable environmental impact, not just technological promise. The water and carbon savings are direct results of precision application: drones apply chemicals and nutrients only where needed, in precisely calibrated amounts, eliminating the over-application that has historically plagued conventional farming.

## Regulatory Tailwinds

One of the report's most significant findings is the global trend toward regulatory liberalization and standardization for agricultural drones. This isn't happening by accident — it's the result of sustained advocacy, field trial data, and the accumulation of operational safety records that regulators can no longer ignore.

**Brazil's ANAC** (National Civil Aviation Agency) updated its drone regulations to establish "standard scenarios" for recurring agricultural operations. This means Brazilian farmers can now conduct routine spraying, mapping, and monitoring under streamlined approval processes rather than case-by-case exemptions.

**Canada's Transport Canada** has similarly amended its Canadian Aviation Regulations to simplify operational rules for agricultural drones, directly supporting spraying, mapping, monitoring, and precision farming activities.

These regulatory shifts matter because they remove the single biggest barrier to agricultural drone adoption: uncertainty about whether operators can legally fly. When farmers know they can operate within clear, predictable rules, investment decisions become straightforward.

## The Science Is Catching Up

The report also highlights a growing body of field trials and academic studies validating agricultural drone advantages across multiple dimensions: precision application, operational efficiency, economic benefits, and sustainability.

Organizations like **UAPASTF** (Unmanned Aircraft Systems Precision Application Spray Technology Foundation) have developed guidelines for safe and effective pesticide application, informed by updated drone field-drift studies. Better drift testing enables more precise, safer, and more compliant field operations — making it a key enabler of precision agriculture and environmentally responsible crop protection.

This evidence base is critical. Early agricultural drone adoption was driven by early adopters willing to experiment. The next wave of growth depends on conservative farmers and institutional buyers who need peer-reviewed data and regulatory confidence before committing capital. The 2025/2026 report suggests that evidence threshold is now being met.

## What This Means for Farmers Considering Drones

If you're evaluating agricultural drones for your operation, the DJI report offers several practical takeaways:

**The technology is past the early-adopter phase.** With 600,000 units in the field and a global support network, agricultural drones are now infrastructure, not experiment. The risk of being stranded with unsupported equipment has diminished significantly.

**Regulatory clarity is improving globally.** From Brazil to Canada to across Asia Pacific, governments are creating predictable frameworks for agricultural drone operations. This trend is likely to continue as safety data accumulates and economic benefits become undeniable.

**Environmental benefits are real and measurable.** The 410 million tons of water saved and 51 million tons of carbon cut aren't abstract claims — they're calculated from operational data across hundreds of thousands of machines. For farmers facing increasing pressure to demonstrate sustainability, drones offer a tangible path forward.

**Training infrastructure exists.** DJI's 7,000 certified instructors represent a global knowledge base that can train operators to proficiency. The learning curve for drone operation is real, but it's no longer an insurmountable barrier.

**ROI timelines are shortening.** As drone costs decrease and operational efficiency improves, the payback period for agricultural drone investment continues to compress. For medium and large operations, the math increasingly favors adoption.

## The Bigger Picture: Agricultural Drones as Climate Tech

Perhaps the most underappreciated aspect of agricultural drone adoption is its climate implications. Precision agriculture — applying exactly what's needed, exactly where it's needed — reduces chemical runoff, minimizes water waste, and cuts the carbon emissions associated with over-application and repeated tractor passes.

DJI's report frames agricultural drones explicitly as climate technology, and the data supports that positioning. The 51 million tons of carbon emissions cut is a meaningful contribution to agricultural decarbonization, achieved not through sacrifice but through efficiency.

As global food systems face dual pressures to increase production and reduce environmental impact, technologies that deliver both simultaneously become indispensable. Agricultural drones are increasingly looking like one of those rare solutions that satisfies both sides of the equation.

## Bottom Line

DJI's 600,000-unit milestone isn't just a corporate achievement — it's a marker of sectoral transformation. Agricultural drones have crossed the chasm from novelty to necessity, from experiment to infrastructure, from promise to measurable impact.

Brazil's experience demonstrates what this transformation looks like in practice: reduced chemical use, eliminated soil compaction, lower carbon footprints, and higher operational efficiency across diverse crop types. The regulatory frameworks emerging globally suggest this isn't a temporary phenomenon — it's the new baseline for modern agriculture.

For farmers, agronomists, and agricultural investors, the question is no longer whether drones will play a role in future farming. The question is how quickly operations can adapt to capture the advantages that 600,000 machines have already proven possible.
