The Federal Aviation Administration has reopened the comment period for what industry observers call the most important drone regulation in years — the proposed rule governing Beyond Visual Line of Sight (BVLOS) operations. The move, announced in early 2026, signals that the FAA is grappling with two of the most contentious technical and policy questions in unmanned aviation: who has the right of way, and how drones must detect and avoid other aircraft.

For the commercial drone industry, this rule represents the difference between niche operations and scalable deployment. BVLOS is the capability that enables drone delivery networks, long-range infrastructure inspection, agricultural mapping across vast acreage, and emergency response beyond the operator's field of view. Without clear BVLOS rules, these applications remain constrained to experimental waivers and small-scale pilots.

## Why the FAA Reopened Comments

The original Notice of Proposed Rulemaking (NPRM) closed its comment period on October 6, 2025. The FAA received extensive feedback from manufacturers, operators, aviation associations, and other stakeholders. Rather than finalizing the rule based on that input, the agency determined that two specific areas warranted additional scrutiny:

**Right-of-Way Frameworks**
The proposed rule established hierarchies determining which aircraft must yield in potential conflict situations. Traditional manned aviation, emergency response aircraft, and unmanned systems all have competing claims to airspace priority. Stakeholders submitted conflicting views on whether the proposed hierarchy appropriately balances safety with operational feasibility.

**Detect-and-Avoid Requirements**
The technical standards for how BVLOS drones must identify and avoid other aircraft proved particularly divisive. The NPRM proposed requiring drones in higher-risk environments — Class B and C airspace or operations over populated areas — to detect "non-cooperative" aircraft (those not broadcasting position via ADS-B Out or equivalent equipment). Industry comments revealed fundamental disagreements about technical feasibility, cost implications, and performance standards.

## What's at Stake

The BVLOS rule isn't just another regulatory document. It determines the operational ceiling for the entire commercial drone industry:

**Delivery at Scale**
Companies like Amazon Prime Air, Wing (Alphabet), and Zipline have built sophisticated drone delivery systems that work — but only within the constraints of current waivers and experimental authorizations. A clear BVLOS framework would enable expansion from pilot neighborhoods to city-wide networks.

**Infrastructure Inspection**
Power lines, pipelines, railways, and telecommunications networks span thousands of miles. Visual-line-ofight operations require inspectors to physically travel alongside the infrastructure being inspected, dramatically increasing cost and time. BVLOS enables efficient, comprehensive coverage.

**Emergency Response**
Search and rescue, wildfire monitoring, and disaster assessment often require operations far from the pilot's location. Current regulations force emergency responders to choose between compliance and operational effectiveness in time-critical situations.

**Agricultural Efficiency**
Large-scale farming operations in the American Midwest and Great Plains cover areas where visual-line-of-sight operations are impractical. BVLOS would enable the kind of comprehensive crop monitoring and precision application that drones promise but can't yet deliver at scale.

## The Technical Challenges

The detect-and-avoid debate centers on genuinely difficult engineering problems:

**Cooperative vs. Non-Cooperative Aircraft**
Most modern manned aircraft broadcast their position via ADS-B Out, making them relatively easy for drone systems to detect electronically. But not all aircraft are equipped — particularly older general aviation planes, helicopters in some configurations, and ultralight aircraft. Detecting these "non-cooperative" targets requires onboard sensors: radar, optical cameras, or acoustic systems.

**Sensor Performance and Cost**
The FAA's proposed requirement for non-cooperative aircraft detection in higher-risk environments would add significant cost and weight to commercial drones. Industry comments have questioned whether available sensor technology can reliably meet the proposed performance standards at price points that enable viable business models.

**False Positive Rates**
An overly sensitive detect-and-avoid system that triggers unnecessary avoidance maneuvers creates its own safety and operational problems. The FAA must balance sensitivity (detecting real threats) against specificity (avoiding false alarms that disrupt legitimate operations).

**Right-of-Way Complexity**
Airspace right-of-way rules evolved over decades of manned aviation. Applying them to drones — which can hover, change altitude rapidly, and operate at speeds and altitudes unfamiliar to traditional pilots — requires more than simple adaptation. The FAA's proposed framework attempts to create drone-specific hierarchies while maintaining compatibility with existing aviation culture.

## Industry Positions

The reopened comment period has drawn predictable battle lines:

**Drone Manufacturers**
Companies like Skydio, DJI, and American-based manufacturers generally support BVLOS authorization but have pushed back on specific detect-and-avoid requirements they consider technically premature or economically impractical. Several manufacturers have submitted alternative technical proposals based on their own research and development.

**Traditional Aviation**
Pilot associations, including the Aircraft Owners and Pilots Association (AOPA), have emphasized the need for robust safety margins. Their comments generally support stringent detect-and-avoid requirements and conservative right-of-way hierarchies that prioritize manned aircraft.

**Commercial Operators**
Companies seeking to deploy BVLOS operations at scale — delivery services, inspection contractors, agricultural service providers — have advocated for performance-based standards rather than prescriptive equipment requirements. Their argument: if a system can demonstrate equivalent safety, it shouldn't matter what specific technology achieves it.

**Air Traffic Management**
The FAA's own air traffic organization has raised integration concerns, particularly about how BVLOS operations will interact with existing traffic management systems and whether new infrastructure (ground-based radar, communication networks) will be required.

## Timeline and Next Steps

The reopened comment period closes February 11, 2026 — though extensions are common for significant rulemakings. After comments close, the FAA faces a lengthy review process:

- **Comment analysis**: 3-6 months to review and categorize submissions
- **Draft final rule preparation**: 6-12 months to incorporate changes
- **Interagency review**: 3-6 months for coordination with Department of Transportation, Department of Homeland Security, and other agencies
- **Publication and effective date**: Final rules typically include 12-24 month implementation periods

Industry observers don't expect a final rule before late 2027, with full implementation potentially stretching into 2029.

## The Global Context

The United States isn't alone in wrestling with BVLOS regulation:

**European Union**
EASA's Specific Category operations under SORA (Specific Operations Risk Assessment) already provide a pathway for BVLOS in Europe, though the process remains cumbersome and case-specific. The EU is watching the FAA rulemaking as a potential model for streamlining its own framework.

**United Kingdom**
The UK Civil Aviation Authority has been more aggressive in authorizing BVLOS through its "operational safety case" approach, granting permissions to several operators for extended-range operations. UK experience is informing FAA thinking, particularly on performance-based standards.

**Australia and Canada**
Both countries are in earlier stages of BVLOS rule development, generally following regulatory models established by the US and EU. The FAA's final rule will likely influence their approaches.

## What Operators Should Watch

For commercial drone operators planning BVLOS operations, the reopened comment period creates both uncertainty and opportunity:

**Participate in Comments**
Operators with relevant experience should consider submitting comments, particularly on practical operational impacts of the proposed right-of-way and detect-and-avoid requirements. The FAA has shown responsiveness to operator feedback in previous rulemakings.

**Plan for Multiple Scenarios**
Given the uncertainty around final requirements, operators should develop business plans that work under several regulatory outcomes — from permissive performance-based standards to stringent equipment mandates.

**Monitor Waiver Opportunities**
Even before the final rule, the FAA continues granting Part 107 waivers for BVLOS operations. Companies with strong safety cases and operational experience can continue expanding through the waiver process while awaiting comprehensive rules.

## Bottom Line

The reopened BVLOS comment period reflects the FAA's recognition that this rule will shape the American drone industry for the next decade. The questions around right-of-way and detect-and-avoid aren't technical niceties — they're the fundamental safety and operational parameters that determine whether commercial drones can operate at scale.

For an industry that has spent years waiting for comprehensive BVLOS authorization, the delay is frustrating. But the alternative — a poorly constructed rule that creates safety incidents or operational impossibilities — would be worse. The FAA's willingness to seek additional input, rather than rushing to finalize, suggests a genuine effort to get this right.

The drone industry should engage constructively in this comment period. The stakes are too high to sit on the sidelines.
